The Innovation

What Carbonix is

Carbonix is a modular emissions-neutralization concept designed to be installed in an aircraft's APU (Auxilary Power Unit) exhaust flow path during ground operations. It is designed around (1) catalytic conversion, (2) uniform flow distribution, and (3) pressure-loss awareness.

  • Target users: airports, airlines, ground operations teams
  • When it matters most: taxi, idle, and gate time
  • Constraint we’re solving: reduce harmful emissions without major aircraft redesign

Problem

Aircraft ground operations create concentrated emissions close to people (workers + nearby communities, putting them at risk).

Solution

A compact catalytic module concept that aims to convert harmful exhaust components into less harmful outputs under ground conditions.

What’s different

Designed as a retrofit-first package with flow distribution + low restriction as core constraints, rather than simply chemistry claims.

The Problem

Why ground emissions matter

Emissions near terminals and taxiways matter because they happen at ground level, close to airport workers and surrounding communities. Small per-aircraft improvements can scale significantly across a busy airport.

  • Where exposure happens: gates, ramps, taxiways
  • Who is exposed: ground crews, passengers, nearby neighborhoods
  • Why it’s hard: real-world constraints (space, heat, pressure loss, maintenance)

How it Works

Technical overview

  • 1) Inlet + conditioning: exhaust enters and is guided for even distribution and liquid cooling.
  • 2) Catalyst core: catalyst leads to the conversion reactions for target gases.
  • 3) Outlet + verification: exhaust exits; sensors/testing verify performance (planned).
Flow distribution Pressure drop Thermal constraints Maintainability

Plain-English explanation

Think of Carbonix as a “filtration system,” except rather then trapping gases, it uses a catalyst to help transform harmful components into less harmful outputs while trying not to choke the exhaust flow.

  • Install: retrofit module concept
  • Run: passive during ground operations
  • Maintain: replace/inspect core on a schedule

Progress & Validation

What we have now

  • CAD model + system architecture
  • Materials/catalyst shortlist (research)
  • Constraints defined (heat, space, pressure loss, maintenance)

Next validation steps

  • Bench testing for pressure drop vs. flow
  • Small-scale catalyst conversion testing in controlled setup with physical prototype
  • Iterate system geometry for uniform distribution

Big risks

Pressure loss, temperature compatibility, catalyst degradation, integration constraints.

Mitigations

Low-restriction design, modular replacement, staged testing, conservative claims.

Intended Impact

Who it helps

Ground crews, passengers, and nearby communities exposed to airport emissions.

Why it matters

Ground-level emissions occur where people are most exposed; mitigation here can be high leverage.

Why adopt

Retrofit concept could offer measurable improvement without waiting for full fleet redesign.

Impact statement

Carbonix aims to reduce harmful aircraft ground emissions by enabling catalytic conversion in a compact, low-restriction retrofit concept, improving air quality around airports.

What success looks like

  • Stakeholder-fit story (airline/airport/regulator)

Adoption concept (edit this)

Pilot program with one airport/partner → limited deployment → iterate based on maintenance + performance results → scale with manufacturing plan.

What Makes Carbonix Different

1) Retrofit-first packaging

Designed around space/heat constraints instead of a lab-only catalyst idea.

2) Flow distribution focus

Geometry aims to avoid channeling so the catalyst is actually used effectively.

3) Pressure-loss awareness

Designed to minimize restriction so it’s more feasible for real operations.

FAQ

Is Carbonix a prototype or a concept?

Currently, Carbonix is a concept.

What emissions does Carbonix target?

The catalyst in Carbonix, anticipated to be Platinum, Palladium, and Rhodium, will target CO (Carbon Monoxide) and NOx (Nitric Oxide), which are harmful toxins.

What are the biggest engineering challenges?

Pressure loss, heat, durability, maintenance cycle, and integration constraints.

How will you prove it works?

Bench tests + controlled conversion measurements + iterative design changes.
